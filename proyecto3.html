High Power Rocket Development for Tripolli Certification (2023)

Laguna Salada, B.C., Mx.

Description

ARLISS is a competition that takes place in Black Rock, Nevada, where university students from around the world design, build, and launch prototypes of satellites. The category where I participated was CanSat Comeback Competition, where the goal is to make a lightweight, all terrain rover that that will be launch from a rocket and will be ejected at an altitude of 4 km. The robot has to autonomously deploy a parachute, land and travel the dessert to a specific GPS location.

My Contribution

Under the engineering student group "Quantum", I contributed to the project as a team member in researching, testing and implementing the necessary manufacturing techniques to construct and assemble a glass reinforced epoxy rocket.

Design through OpenRocket software for calculating center of mass, center of pressure, weight distribution, motor selection and conducting flight simulations to ensure a stable launch and landing.

Through a process of trial and error, I was able to identify certain deficiencies in the manufacturing process and successfully proposed and implemented changes to mitigate them.

Results

Success! The launch took place over laguna salada in Mexicali, B.C, Mexico. The flight passed with flying colors all certification criteria established by Tripolli Rocketry Association.

  • Rocket didn't explode. Happens more often than one might think. If materials are not chosen correctly for sustaining high pressure and heat generated by motor ignition, the rocket would most likely explode.
  • Flight trajectory. Flight path needed to be straight up, no sudden turns or trajectory changes. This meant a good fin design and adequate CM and CP.
  • Correct flight path and parachute deployment. Parachute needed to be deployed at periapsis and not be burned by motor heat. If parachute deployed too early it would most likely brake. If deployed too late, it wouldn't slow down rocket trajectory enough and would most likely result in a complete loss of the rocket.
